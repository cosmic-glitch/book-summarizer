Book Summary: Sapiens

An Animal of No Significance

  • The Evolution of Humans:
    • Humans evolved from earlier ape species, including Australopithecus, over 2.5 million years ago.
    • Several human species, including Homo neanderthalensis, Homo erectus, and Homo floresiensis, coexisted for thousands of years.
    • Homo sapiens emerged around 150,000 years ago and eventually spread out of Africa, encountering and potentially interbreeding with other human species.
  • Characteristics of Early Humans:
    • Early humans had significantly larger brains compared to other animals, but this came at a cost in terms of energy consumption and muscle development.
    • Upright walking freed the hands for tool use, but also caused challenges like narrower hips and more difficult childbirth.
    • Humans were born relatively underdeveloped, allowing for greater socialization and education compared to other animals.
  • The Significance of Fire:
    • The domestication of fire, around 300,000 years ago, was a major turning point, allowing humans to cook food, stay warm, and burn down forests.
    • Cooking food enabled humans to get more energy from their diet, potentially contributing to the growth of the human brain.
    • Fire gave humans a powerful tool that was not limited by the physical constraints of the human body, setting them apart from other animals.
  • The Fate of Other Human Species:
    • Theories differ on whether Homo sapiens interbreeded with other human species like Neanderthals, or simply replaced them.
    • Genetic evidence suggests some interbreeding occurred, but the extent is still debated.
    • The disappearance of other human species remains a mystery, with possibilities ranging from competition for resources to outright genocide by Homo sapiens.
  • The Uniqueness of Homo Sapiens:
    • Homo sapiens' success in conquering the world and outcompeting other human species is likely due to our unique language abilities.
    • The loss of our human relatives raises questions about how human evolution and history might have unfolded differently if multiple human species had coexisted.

The Tree of Knowledge

  • Archaic Sapiens vs. Modern Humans:
    • For a long time, archaic Sapiens did not have any marked advantages over other human species and did not produce sophisticated tools or accomplishments.
    • Around 70,000 years ago, there was a "Cognitive Revolution" that enabled Sapiens to think in unprecedented ways and communicate using a new type of language.
  • Language and the Ability to Imagine Fictions:
    • Sapiens' unique language allowed them to communicate about things that do not exist, like legends, myths, and religions.
    • This ability to create and believe in imagined realities enabled large-scale human cooperation and the development of complex societies and institutions.
  • Bypassing Genetic Evolution:
    • While animal behavior is largely determined by genetics, Sapiens could rapidly transform their social structures and behaviors through cultural evolution.
    • This allowed Sapiens to far outstrip other human and animal species in their ability to cooperate and adapt to changing environments.
  • The Relationship Between Biology and History:
    • Biology sets the basic parameters for Sapiens, but the ability to create imagined realities allows an astounding variety of cultural and historical developments within those parameters.
    • Understanding Sapiens' behavior requires describing the historical evolution of their actions, not just their biological constraints.

A Day in the Life of Adam and Eve

  • Forager Lifestyle and Economy:
    • Sapiens lived as hunter-gatherers for most of human history, with a varied diet and relatively short work week compared to later agricultural and industrial societies.
    • Foragers had extensive knowledge of their local environment and the natural world, with high levels of physical fitness and dexterity.
    • The forager economy was generally more comfortable and rewarding than later modes of subsistence, providing adequate nutrition and fewer chores.
  • Spiritual and Mental Life:
    • Animistic beliefs were common among ancient foragers, with the view that non-human entities like animals, plants, and natural phenomena have agency and can communicate with humans.
    • The specifics of forager religions and worldviews are largely unknown due to the scarcity of evidence from that time period.
  • Sociopolitical Structure:
    • The sociopolitical organization of forager societies is difficult to determine, with disagreement among scholars about the prevalence of private property, nuclear families, and hierarchies.
    • Archaeological evidence suggests some forager societies were highly stratified, with burials indicating significant social inequality.
  • Violence and Conflict:
    • The level of violence and warfare among ancient foragers is controversial, with some evidence of high rates of interpersonal violence in certain regions, while other areas appear more peaceful.
    • The archaeological record is limited and difficult to interpret, making it challenging to generalize about the prevalence of violent conflict.
  • Limitations of Knowledge:
    • The vast majority of forager history remains unknown, with a "curtain of silence" obscuring most details about their beliefs, cultures, and political events.
    • Scholars can only make cautious generalizations based on the limited evidence available, without being able to reconstruct the specifics of ancient forager life.

The Flood

  • Pre-Cognitive Revolution Humans:
    • Lived exclusively on the Afro-Asian landmass, unable to venture into the open sea.
    • Prevented from reaching distant lands like Australia, America, and remote islands.
    • Resulted in distinct ecosystems evolving in isolation for millions of years.
  • Cognitive Revolution and Colonization of Australia:
    • Humans developed seafaring abilities and reached Australia about 45,000 years ago.
    • Crossing wide sea channels and adapting to a new ecosystem was an unprecedented feat.
    • Humans became the deadliest species, transforming the Australian ecosystem beyond recognition.
  • Extinction of Australian Megafauna:
    • Within a few thousand years, 23 out of 24 Australian animal species weighing over 50kg became extinct.
    • Evidence implicates human hunting, fire agriculture, and climate change as the causes.
    • Humans were the ecological serial killers, causing similar extinctions in other regions they colonized.
  • Colonization and Extinction in the Americas:
    • Humans arrived in the Americas about 16,000 years ago, spreading rapidly across the continent.
    • Resulted in the extinction of 34 out of 47 genera of large mammals in North America and 50 out of 60 in South America.
    • Evidence, such as fossilized dung, clearly implicates human involvement in these extinctions.
  • The Ecological Disaster:
    • The first wave of human colonization was one of the biggest ecological disasters, driving half the planet's large mammals to extinction.
    • Similar extinctions occurred on islands as humans spread, with many unique species disappearing.
    • Humans have the dubious distinction of being the deadliest species in the history of biology.

History's Biggest Fraud

  • The Transition to Agriculture:
    • For 2.5 million years, humans lived as hunter-gatherers, feeding themselves by gathering plants and hunting animals.
    • Around 10,000 years ago, humans began devoting most of their time and effort to manipulating the lives of a few animal and plant species - the Agricultural Revolution.
    • This revolution began around 9500-8500 BC in the Middle East and spread independently to other parts of the world.
    • The main domesticated plants that feed humanity today were domesticated between 9500 and 3500 BC.
  • The Myth of Progress:
    • Scholars once believed the Agricultural Revolution was a great leap forward for humanity, driven by increasing human intelligence.
    • However, there is no evidence that people became more intelligent over time. Foragers already knew the secrets of nature.
    • The Agricultural Revolution did not lead to an easier, more satiated life. Farmers generally had a harder and less satisfying existence than foragers.
    • The Agricultural Revolution was "history's biggest fraud" - it enlarged the food supply but did not translate into better diets or more leisure.
  • Wheat Domesticating Humans:
    • Wheat and other plants, rather than humans, were the real agents behind the Agricultural Revolution.
    • Wheat manipulated humans to its advantage, leading them to invest immense effort in its cultivation.
    • Wheat did not offer a better diet, economic security, or protection from violence - it simply enabled human population growth.
  • The Luxury Trap:
    • The transition to agriculture was a gradual process, with each small change seeming like an improvement.
    • However, these changes accumulated, trapping humans in an increasingly difficult existence.
    • Humans were unable to foresee the full consequences of their decisions, as luxuries became necessities, spawning new obligations.
  • Divine Intervention?
    • Evidence from sites like Göbekli Tepe suggests the Agricultural Revolution may have been driven by religious or ideological motivations, not just the search for an easier life.
    • The construction of monumental structures by pre-agricultural foragers indicates a level of social complexity and cooperation that challenges conventional views.
    • Göbekli Tepe's proximity to the origins of domesticated wheat suggests a connection between the temple and the initial domestication of this crucial crop.
  • The Victims of the Revolution:
    • Domesticated animals like sheep, goats, pigs, and chickens were also victims of the Agricultural Revolution.
    • Humans systematically altered the genetic makeup of these animals through selective breeding and cruel practices to suit their own needs.
    • From the perspective of individual animal welfare, the Agricultural Revolution was often a "terrible catastrophe," even as it enabled the exponential growth of these species.

Building Pyramids

  • The Agricultural Revolution's Impact:
    • Farming enabled rapid population growth, with 250 million farmers by the 1st century AD compared to 5-8 million foragers before the transition.
    • Settlements shrank from vast territories to small artificial human islands surrounded by untamed nature.
    • Attachment to "my house" and separation from neighbors became a psychological hallmark.
  • The Coming of the Future:
    • Foragers lived in the present, but farmers had to plan for the future to deal with seasonal cycles and uncertainty in agriculture.
    • Farmers became anxious and hardworking, producing food surpluses to build reserves against bad years.
    • The stress of farming fueled the rise of large-scale political and social systems, with peasants supporting elites through their food surpluses.
  • An Imagined Order:
    • Food surpluses and improved transportation enabled the growth of cities, kingdoms, and empires, but these required new cooperation networks sustained by shared myths.
    • Myths like the Code of Hammurabi and the American Declaration of Independence provided the basis for mass cooperation, though the principles they espoused were not objectively true.
    • Equality, rights, and other imagined principles are not biologically real, but serve as useful fictions to enable large-scale cooperation.
  • True Believers:
    • Imagined orders depend on continuous efforts to sustain them, including violence and coercion.
    • But true believers, particularly among the elite and security forces, are also necessary to maintain the imagined order.
    • The elite must believe in the order, not just cynically enforce it, for it to persist.

Memory Overload

  • The Limitations of the Human Brain:
    • The human brain is not well-suited for storing and processing the massive amounts of information required to maintain large-scale human social networks and empires.
    • Humans lack the innate, instinctual abilities of other social animals like ants and bees to encode critical information in their DNA and pass it on.
    • The human brain has a limited capacity and information stored in it is vulnerable to being forgotten or distorted over time.
    • The human brain is adapted to store botanical, zoological, and social information, but not large amounts of mathematical data vital for governing complex societies.
  • The Invention of Writing:
    • The ancient Sumerians invented writing around 3500-3000 BC to overcome the limitations of the human brain.
    • Writing combined numerical signs and signs representing people, objects, and concepts to store and process large amounts of data.
    • Early writing was a "partial script" focused on recording facts and figures, rather than a "full script" capable of expressing the full range of human language and thought.
    • Other civilizations, like the Andes, also developed partial script systems using technologies like quipus (knotted cords) to record data.
  • The Rise of Bureaucracy:
    • As archives of written records grew, new organizational methods were needed to catalog and retrieve information efficiently.
    • Scribes and administrators were trained to think in a compartmentalized, non-human way to manage these bureaucratic systems.
    • The development of mathematical notation, including Hindu-Arabic numerals, further empowered data processing and storage.
    • Increasingly, human thought and expression is being translated into the language of numbers legible to computers, rather than traditional human language.

There Is No Justice in History

  • Hierarchies and Fictional Origins:
    • All complex human societies require imagined hierarchies, which often claim to be natural and inevitable.
    • Hierarchies like slavery, racism, and caste systems are rooted in fiction and human invention, not biological reality.
    • Ruling classes use concepts of purity and pollution to justify and enforce social divisions.
  • The Vicious Circle of Race in America:
    • Circumstantial factors, like the slave trade and disease immunity, led to the racial hierarchy in America.
    • Racist myths and ideologies were used to justify slavery and segregation, perpetuating a cycle of discrimination.
    • As blacks remained disadvantaged, it reinforced beliefs about their inferiority, leading to more discrimination.
  • The Hierarchy of Gender:
    • The division of humans into men and women, with men getting the better deal, has been a universal hierarchy.
    • Women were often considered the property of men, with rape viewed as a violation of the man's property rights.
    • Even in modern societies, gender hierarchies and discrimination against women have persisted.
  • The Persistence of Imagined Hierarchies:
    • Unjust discrimination often gets worse, not better, over time, as vicious circles perpetuate imagined hierarchies.
    • Understanding the historical origins and social construction of hierarchies is crucial, as biology alone cannot explain them.

The Arrow of History

  • The Changing Nature of Cultures:
    • Cultures were once believed to have a fixed, unchanging essence, but scholars now recognize they are in constant flux.
    • Cultures transform in response to environmental changes, interactions with other cultures, and internal contradictions within the culture.
    • Contradictions within cultures, such as between Christian values and chivalric ideals, drive cultural change and creativity.
  • The Trend Towards Global Unity:
    • Over millennia, small, simple cultures have gradually coalesced into larger, more complex civilizations.
    • Around 10,000 BC, there were thousands of isolated human worlds, but by 1450 AD, 90% of humans lived in the Afro-Asian mega-world.
    • The remaining worlds were eventually conquered and absorbed by the Afro-Asian giant over the next 300 years.
    • Today, almost all humans share the same geopolitical, economic, legal, and scientific systems, forming a single global culture.
  • The Rise of Universal Orders:
    • The first millennium BC saw the emergence of three potential universal orders: the monetary, imperial, and religious orders.
    • Merchants, conquerors, and prophets were the first to transcend the "us vs. them" mentality and envision the potential unity of humankind.
    • Over the past three millennia, people have made increasingly ambitious attempts to realize this global vision.

The Scent of Money

  • The Spanish Conquistadors' Obsession with Gold:
    • The Aztecs were puzzled by the Spanish obsession with gold, a metal with no practical use.
    • The Spanish saw gold as a cure for a "disease of the heart" that afflicted them.
    • This passion for gold was widespread in the Afro-Asian world the Spanish came from, even among bitter religious enemies.
  • The Rise of Money as a Medium of Exchange:
    • Hunter-gatherers and early agriculturalists relied on barter and favors, but as economies grew more complex, money became necessary.
    • Money can take many forms, from cowry shells to cigarettes, that people are willing to accept in exchange.
    • Money enables the conversion, storage, and transportation of wealth, facilitating complex commercial networks and markets.
  • The Nature of Money:
    • Money is a psychological construct based on trust, not an inherent material value.
    • Early money like Sumerian barley had intrinsic value, but the breakthrough was in money lacking inherent value but being easier to store and transport, like silver shekels.
    • Coins with standardized weights and identifying marks from authorities helped build trust in money.
  • The Globalization of Monetary Systems:
    • Lydian-style coinage spread from the Mediterranean to the Indian Ocean, creating a single monetary zone.
    • The shared belief in gold and silver facilitated global trade and the unification of the world economy.
    • But money also erodes local traditions and intimate human relations, as everything becomes convertible to cash.

Imperial Visions

  • Numantia as a Symbol of Spanish Independence:
    • Numantia, an ancient Celtic town in Spain, became a symbol of Spanish independence and courage after its destruction by the Romans.
    • The town's fierce defenders were celebrated in poetry, art, and popular culture, and its ruins were declared a national monument.
    • However, the Numantians spoke a lost Celtic language, and their story has reached us through the writings of Roman historians, who tailored it to the tastes of Roman audiences.
  • The Nature of Empires:
    • Empires are defined by their cultural diversity and flexible borders, allowing them to unite diverse ethnic groups and ecological zones under a single political umbrella.
    • Empires have been the dominant form of political organization for the last 2,500 years, and are often remarkably stable, able to suppress rebellions and conquer subject peoples.
    • The destruction of one empire rarely leads to the independence of subject peoples, as a new empire often steps in to fill the vacuum.
  • The Brutality and Legacy of Empires:
    • Building and maintaining an empire often involved the vicious slaughter and brutal oppression of conquered populations.
    • However, empires have also financed significant cultural achievements, and their legacies continue to shape the languages, laws, and customs of most modern cultures.
    • Imperial ideologies have often justified conquest as being for the benefit of the conquered, portraying the empire as a benevolent, inclusive, and universal project.
  • The Acculturation and Assimilation of Subject Peoples:
    • Empires have played a major role in amalgamating small cultures into fewer, larger ones, often deliberately spreading common ideas, institutions, and norms.
    • This process of acculturation and assimilation was often painful and traumatic for the vanquished, who had to give up their familiar local traditions.
    • However, in some cases, the conquered eventually came to see the imperial culture as their own, and were accepted as equals by the ruling elite.
  • The Imperial Cycle:
    • This pattern of imperial conquest, acculturation, and assimilation has repeated itself across history, from the Roman Empire to European colonialism.
    • Just as subject peoples of the past adopted and adapted the imperial cultures of their conquerors, modern decolonization movements have claimed equality in the name of the very Western values and ideologies that were spread by European empires.

The Law of Religion

  • The Rise of Universal and Missionary Religions:
    • Religions like Islam and Buddhism emerged as universal and missionary faiths, aiming to convert the entire human race.
    • This was a major revolution in history, contributing to the unification of humankind.
    • Previous local and exclusive religions primarily focused on local deities and had no interest in converting others.
  • The Transition from Animism to Polytheism:
    • The Agricultural Revolution led to plants and animals being seen as human possessions rather than equal spiritual beings.
    • Gods like the fertility goddess and the sky god gained importance, mediating between humans and the mute natural world.
    • Polytheistic religions emerged, with a pantheon of powerful gods that humans could appeal to for favors.
  • The Emergence of Monotheism:
    • Some followers of polytheistic gods came to believe their god was the one supreme deity, giving rise to monotheistic religions.
    • Christianity and Islam were highly successful monotheistic faiths that spread rapidly, often violently suppressing other religions.
    • Monotheists tended to be more fanatical and missionary than polytheists, seeing their faith as the sole universal truth.
  • The Persistence of Polytheism and Animism:
    • Even within monotheism, polytheistic and animistic beliefs and practices continued to thrive, leading to syncretism.
    • Christians, Muslims, and Jews incorporated saints, spirits, and dualistic concepts like heaven and hell into their faiths.
  • The Rise of Natural-Law Religions:
    • Religions like Buddhism and Daoism emerged during the first millennium BC, rejecting belief in gods and instead focusing on universal natural laws.
    • Buddhism, in particular, saw suffering as arising from innate human mental patterns, not divine whims, and emphasized meditation and ethical training to attain liberation.

The Secret of Success

  • The Hindsight Fallacy:
    • History is not deterministic - many possible paths exist, and the one that was realized was not inevitable.
    • What seems obvious in hindsight was often highly unlikely at the time, like the rise of Christianity or Bolshevism.
    • Historians are more aware of the roads not taken when they have deep knowledge of a historical period.
    • The future is always uncertain, and predictions about it are often wrong.
  • Blind Clio:
    • The choices made by history are not necessarily for the benefit of humans.
    • There is no objective scale to measure whether a culture or belief system is "better" for humanity.
    • Cultures can be seen as "mental parasites" that propagate themselves with little regard for human well-being.
    • The dynamics of history, like evolution, are not directed towards enhancing human happiness.
    • Individuals are usually too ignorant and weak to influence the course of history to their own advantage.
  • The Scientific Revolution:
    • The Scientific Revolution, beginning around 1500 in Western Europe, was a pivotal moment in history.
    • Scholars do not fully understand why the Scientific Revolution began when and where it did, rather than in China or India.
    • History has a wide range of possibilities, and many possibilities are never realized.

The Discovery of Ignorance

  • The Scientific Revolution's Feedback Loop:
    • The last 500 years have witnessed phenomenal growth in human population, production, and energy consumption.
    • The Scientific Revolution is a revolution of ignorance, not just of knowledge.
    • Modern science is based on the willingness to admit ignorance, the centrality of observation and mathematics, and the acquisition of new powers.
  • The Willingness to Admit Ignorance:
    • Premodern traditions asserted that everything important to know was already known.
    • Modern science openly admits collective ignorance regarding the most important questions.
    • This willingness to admit ignorance has made modern science more dynamic, supple, and inquisitive than any previous tradition of knowledge.
  • The Centrality of Observation and Mathematics:
    • Modern science aims to obtain new knowledge by gathering observations and using mathematical tools.
    • This is in contrast to premodern traditions, which formulated theories in terms of stories rather than mathematics.
    • The development of statistics has been crucial in enabling the application of mathematical methods to complex fields like biology, economics, and psychology.
  • The Acquisition of New Powers:
    • Modern science is not content with creating theories; it uses these theories to acquire new powers, especially in the form of new technologies.
    • The connection between science and technology is a relatively recent phenomenon, with science often being used to serve political, economic, and military interests.
    • The belief in the ability of science and technology to solve all problems has become a central tenet of modern culture, replacing the traditional reliance on absolute truths.
  • The Gilgamesh Project:
    • The leading project of the Scientific Revolution is to give humankind eternal life, overcoming the age-old problem of death.
    • Advances in medicine, surgery, and public health have already dramatically increased life expectancy, especially by reducing childhood mortality.
    • Some scholars believe that by 2050, some humans may become "a-mortal", with their lives extended indefinitely through technological means.
  • The Sugar Daddy of Science:
    • Science is a very expensive endeavor, requiring significant resources and funding.
    • Throughout history, most rulers and business people did not finance scientific research, focusing instead on preserving traditional knowledge.
    • The tight relationship between science, politics, and economics is a relatively recent development, with modern states and industries heavily investing in scientific research to further their own interests.

The Marriage of Science and Empire

  • Expeditions to Observe Venus Transits:
    • In the 18th century, astronomers predicted that Venus would transit across the sun in 1761 and 1769.
    • Expeditions were sent from Europe to observe the transits from distant locations to calculate the Earth's distance from the sun.
    • The Royal Society of London sent an expedition led by Captain James Cook to observe the 1769 transit from Tahiti.
  • The Cook Expedition:
    • The expedition was a scientific voyage, but it was also a naval expedition supported by the Royal Navy.
    • In addition to astronomers, the expedition included botanists, artists, and a geographer, Captain Cook.
    • The expedition made significant contributions to various scientific disciplines and sparked European interest in the South Pacific.
  • Conquering Scurvy:
    • Scurvy was a major killer of sailors on long voyages before the 18th century.
    • James Lind conducted experiments that showed citrus fruits could cure scurvy, a discovery that Captain Cook put into practice.
    • Cook's successful voyage without losing a single sailor to scurvy led to the adoption of this cure by the world's navies.
  • The Darker Consequences:
    • The Cook expedition laid the foundation for the British colonization of Australia, Tasmania, and New Zealand, leading to the devastation of native populations.
    • The scientific and geographical data collected by the expedition also proved useful for military and political conquest.
    • Science and empire were closely intertwined, as exemplified by the fate of the Tasmanian people.
  • The Rise of European Dominance:
    • Europe's rise to global dominance was not solely due to technological superiority, but also to the development of a unique mindset of exploration and conquest.
    • European maps shifted from depicting a fully known world to leaving empty spaces, inviting further exploration and conquest.
    • Unlike previous empires, European expansion was driven by a desire for new knowledge and a willingness to admit ignorance about much of the world.

The Capitalist Creed

  • The Magic of Credit:
    • Banks can loan out more money than they actually have in deposits, creating new money through credit.
    • This system is based on trust in future economic growth and productivity, rather than tangible assets.
    • Credit enables financing of new enterprises and economic expansion, overcoming the limitations of a static economy.
  • The Emergence of Capitalism:
    • The belief in progress and economic growth replaced the traditional view of a fixed or declining pie of wealth.
    • Adam Smith argued that the pursuit of profits is the basis for collective wealth and prosperity, not a zero-sum game.
    • The capitalist creed holds that profits should be reinvested to increase production, rather than spent on unproductive activities.
  • The Symbiosis of Capitalism and Science:
    • Capitalism relies on continuous scientific and technological breakthroughs to fuel economic growth.
    • Scientific research is often funded based on its potential to increase production and profits.
    • The modern economy depends on scientists and innovators to justify the creation of trillions in "make-believe money".
  • Capitalism and Imperialism:
    • European empires were increasingly financed through credit rather than taxes, driven by capitalist investors seeking profits.
    • Expeditions of discovery and conquest were risky, but the potential for colonial profits convinced investors to provide funding.
    • This created a "magic circle" where credit financed new discoveries, which led to colonies and profits, further building trust and credit.

The Wheels of Industry

  • The Capitalist Creed:
    • Nationalization of colonies by European powers did not end the embrace of capitalism and empire.
    • Governments often did the bidding of big business interests, as seen in the First Opium War and Egypt's debt crisis.
    • Wars were sometimes fought explicitly in the interests of investors, such as the Greek Rebellion Bonds.
    • The connection between capital and politics has had far-reaching implications, influencing a country's creditworthiness and economic well-being.
  • The Cult of the Free Market:
    • Ardent capitalists argue that capital should be free to influence politics, but politics should not interfere with the market.
    • The belief in a completely free market is naive, as markets require political systems to ensure trust and regulate against fraud and theft.
  • The Capitalist Hell:
    • In a completely free market, avaricious capitalists can exploit workers through monopolies, debt peonage, and slavery.
    • The pursuit of profit has led to atrocities like the Atlantic slave trade and the exploitation of the Congo.
    • Capitalism has brought material progress, but also great inequities and human suffering that cannot be ignored.
  • The Industrial Revolution and Energy Conversion:
    • The Industrial Revolution was a revolution in energy conversion, allowing humanity to harness new energy sources beyond muscle power.
    • Inventions like the steam engine, internal combustion engine, and electricity dramatically expanded the energy available for economic growth.
    • Similarly, new materials and technologies enabled the extraction and use of previously inaccessible raw materials.
    • This abundance of energy and raw materials fueled the Second Agricultural Revolution, dramatically increasing agricultural productivity.
    • However, the mechanization of agriculture has also led to the treatment of animals as mere "production machines".

A Permanent Revolution

  • Ecological Degradation vs. Resource Scarcity:
    • Ecological degradation is a growing threat, while resource scarcity predictions are likely exaggerated.
    • Human power and technology could lead to increased disruption of the ecosystem, resulting in unintended consequences.
    • Despite environmental concerns, human population has grown dramatically since the Industrial Revolution.
  • The Transformation of Time and Work:
    • The Industrial Revolution replaced the rhythms of traditional agriculture with a uniform and precise industrial timetable.
    • Factories, schools, and other institutions adopted strict schedules and assembly line methods, changing human behavior.
    • Public transportation and broadcast media helped enforce and spread the new industrial time regime.
  • The Collapse of the Family and Community:
    • Families and local communities were previously the basic building blocks of human society.
    • The state and market have taken over many of the traditional functions of families and communities.
    • Individuals are now seen as independent from families and communities, relying on the state and market for support.
  • The Rise of Imagined Communities:
    • The nation and the consumer tribe are examples of imagined communities, where strangers feel a sense of belonging.
    • These imagined communities have replaced the intimate bonds of traditional communities.
    • Nationalism and consumerism work to make people feel connected to large groups of strangers.
  • A Permanent State of Change:
    • The pace of social change has accelerated dramatically in the modern era.
    • The social order is now characterized by constant flux and the promise of radical transformation.
    • Despite increased potential for violence, the modern world has also seen unprecedented periods of peace and stability.

And They Lived Happily Ever After

  • The Pursuit of Happiness:
    • Historians rarely examine whether human progress has actually increased happiness.
    • Different ideologies have different views on the sources of human happiness, but these are often based on flimsy ideas.
    • Serious research is needed to understand the long-term history of happiness and its determinants.
  • Measuring Happiness:
    • Psychologists and biologists use subjective well-being questionnaires to measure happiness.
    • Wealth, health, and social relations have a positive but limited impact on happiness.
    • Happiness is more strongly correlated with meeting expectations than with objective conditions.
  • The Biochemistry of Happiness:
    • Biologists argue that happiness is primarily determined by biochemical factors like serotonin and dopamine.
    • Humans have a set point of happiness determined by their biochemistry, which historical events cannot easily change.
    • Manipulating biochemistry through drugs may be more effective for increasing happiness than political or social reforms.
  • The Brave New World:
    • Aldous Huxley's novel Brave New World depicts a dystopia where happiness is achieved through psychiatric drugs.
    • This vision raises ethical questions about the value of true happiness versus engineered contentment.

The End of Homo Sapiens

  • The Biological Revolution:
    • For billions of years, all life on Earth evolved through natural selection alone, with no intelligent design.
    • In the last few decades, humans have begun to transcend the limits of natural selection through biological engineering.
    • Techniques like genetic engineering and selective breeding allow us to create organisms that could never arise naturally.
    • Examples include a fluorescent green rabbit, genetically engineered cows and pigs, and laboratory-engineered 'genius mice'.
  • Ethical Concerns:
    • Biological engineering raises ethical, political and ideological issues.
    • Concerns include animal suffering, the creation of 'supermen', and fears of bio-dictatorships.
    • Currently, genetic engineering is mainly used on organisms with weaker political lobbies, like bacteria, plants and insects.
    • Regulations and public opinion have limited the pace of more controversial human-related genetic engineering so far.
  • The Future of Intelligent Design:
    • Biological engineering, cyborg engineering, and the engineering of inorganic life could lead to the complete replacement of natural selection by intelligent design.
    • This would mark a profound shift in the history of life on Earth, with profound implications yet to be fully understood.
