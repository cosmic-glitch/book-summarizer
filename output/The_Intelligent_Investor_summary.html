Book Summary: The Intelligent Investor

Investment versus Speculation: Results to Be Expected by the Intelligent Investor

  • Defining Investment vs Speculation:
    • Investment operation promises safety of principal and adequate return
    • Speculation does not meet these requirements
    • Criticism of calling all stock market participants "investors"
  • Results for the Defensive Investor:
    • Recommends 25-75% allocation between bonds and stocks
    • In 1965, could get 4.5% yield on bonds, 3.2% on stocks
    • Now (1972) bonds yield 7.5%, stocks around 3.5%
    • Bonds appear preferable, but stocks hedge against inflation
    • Suggests 50/50 bonds/stocks with 5.5% expected after-tax return
  • Results for the Aggressive Investor:
    • Trading, short-term selectivity unlikely to yield better results
    • Long-term growth stocks prone to over-valuation
    • Look for unloved, undervalued stocks and special situations
    • These areas less fruitful recently due to increased competition
    • Still opportunities if can add 5%+ to returns vs market averages

The Investor and Inflation

  • Inflation's Impact on Fixed-Income Investments:
    • Inflation erodes purchasing power of fixed dollar income from bonds.
    • This has led some to argue for 100% stock portfolios over bonds.
  • Historical Analysis of Inflation:
    • The U.S. has experienced significant inflation periods in the past.
    • Reasonable to expect future 3% annual inflation based on recent trends.
    • This would reduce real returns on bonds by about half but not necessarily erode principal.
  • Stock Performance in Inflationary Periods:
    • No clear connection between inflation and stock earnings/prices in short-term.
    • Earnings on corporate equity capital have declined over past 20 years despite inflation.
    • Inflation has not increased real profitability due to rising costs and capital needs.
  • Other Inflation Hedges:
    • Gold has underperformed inflation over the long run.
    • Real estate can hedge inflation but has risks and limited diversification.
  • Recommended Portfolio Strategy:
    • Conservative investors should hold both bonds and stocks.
    • Stocks provide some inflation protection but have higher risk than bonds.
    • Balance is needed to minimize risks from unexpected inflation or deflation.

General Portfolio Policy: The Defensive Investor

  • Bond-Stock Allocation:
    • Defensive investors should divide funds between high-grade bonds and high-grade common stocks.
    • Suggested range: 25% to 75% in common stocks, with the rest in bonds.
    • The standard division should be 50-50 between bonds and stocks.
    • Increase stock allocation when prices are low, reduce when prices are high.
    • A 50-50 formula is proposed: rebalance when stock allocation moves above 55% or below 45%.
  • The Bond Component:
    • Choose between taxable or tax-free bonds based on investor's tax bracket.
    • U.S. Savings Bonds (Series E and H) offer unique advantages like tax deferral and redemption flexibility.
    • Other bond choices include U.S. Treasury bonds, municipal bonds, and corporate bonds.
    • Higher yields are available from lower-grade bonds, but defensive investors should avoid them.
    • Bank savings deposits and CDs can substitute for short-term bond investments.
  • Preferred Stocks:
    • Preferred stocks have weaknesses in their legal position compared to bonds and common stocks.
    • Only a small percentage maintain strong investment status through adversity.
    • Preferred stocks should be bought at bargain prices by defensive investors.
  • Security Forms:
    • Income bonds, where interest is paid only if earned, have advantages but are underutilized.
    • Convertible issues and other specialized forms are discussed separately.

The Defensive Investor and Common Stocks

  • Investment Merits of Common Stocks:
    • Common stocks offer protection against inflation and tend to have higher average returns than bonds due to dividend income and underlying growth.
    • However, these benefits can be lost if the investor pays too high a price for the stocks.
  • Rules for the Common-Stock Component:
    • Adequate but not excessive diversification (10-30 different stocks).
    • Each company should be large, prominent, and conservatively financed.
    • Each company should have a long record of continuous dividend payments (at least since 1950).
    • Limit the price paid to 25 times the average earnings over the past 7 years, and 20 times the earnings of the latest 12 months.
  • Growth Stocks and the Defensive Investor:
    • Growth stocks are too risky and speculative for defensive investors due to their high prices relative to earnings.
    • Examples of volatility in growth stocks like IBM and Texas Instruments are provided.
  • Portfolio Changes:
    • Defensive investors should have their portfolios reviewed periodically by reputable advisors.
    • Advisors should adhere to the rules for common-stock selection.
  • Dollar-Cost Averaging:
    • Investing the same dollar amount regularly in stocks can lead to good returns over time.
    • It prevents investing at wrong times and can be done systematically.
  • The Investor's Personal Situation:
    • Specific examples of a widow, doctor, and young man are discussed.
    • The defensive strategy is recommended unless the investor has expertise and temperament to be an "enterprising" investor.

Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: Negative Approach

  • Start from the Same Base as the Defensive Investor:
    • Divide funds between high-grade bonds and reasonably priced high-grade common stocks.
    • Branch out into other investments only with well-reasoned justification.
  • Negative Approach for Enterprising Investors:
    • Avoid high-grade preferred stocks, as they are better suited for corporations.
    • Avoid inferior bonds and preferred stocks unless bought at bargain prices (at least 30% below par for high-coupons).
    • Avoid foreign government bonds, as they lack legal enforceability.
    • Be wary of new issues, especially convertible bonds/preferreds and common stocks with only recent earnings history.
  • Second-Grade Bonds and Preferred Stocks:
    • Not advisable to buy for marginally higher yields when high-grade bonds offer over 7% yield.
    • Susceptible to severe price declines during market downturns, even if earnings are not bad.
    • Some eventually recover when conditions improve, but unpredictable which ones.
  • New Common Stock Offerings:
    • Tend to be of lower quality companies as bull markets progress.
    • Often sold at exorbitant prices driven by public frenzy.
    • Usually followed by severe price collapses of 75% or more.
    • Intelligent investors should resist blandishments of salesmen offering new issues.

Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: The Positive Side

  • General Market Timing:
    • Graham advises against trying to time the market based solely on formulas, as market fluctuations require special trading skills.
    • He suggests the 50-50 plan outlined for defensive investors as the best automatic formula.
  • Growth Stock Approach:
    • Growth stocks tend to be overpriced, and most investors cannot obtain better results from them than professional funds.
    • Graham recommends avoiding growth stocks selling at high P/E ratios (over 20 times average earnings).
  • Three Recommended Approaches:
    1. Buying Relatively Unpopular Large Companies:
      • Look for large, prominent companies selling below their average price/earnings ratios - the "Dogs of the Dow" approach.
    2. Purchase of Bargain Issues:
      • Buy stocks selling at less than 2/3 of their appraised value, or less than net current assets.
      • Also consider bargain bonds and secondary companies selling at depressed prices.
    3. Special Situations or "Workouts":
      • Complex corporate events like mergers, reorganizations, etc. can create bargain opportunities if misunderstood by the market.
      • But this requires highly specialized knowledge and is not for most investors.
  • Implications of the Rules:
    • Most investors should be defensive and avoid riskier securities at full prices.
    • Enterprising investors must view investing as a business to undertake more complex strategies.
    • Following Graham's advice could depress prices of secondary stocks, but this reflects reality.

The Investor and Market Fluctuations

  • Fixed Income Investments and Market Fluctuations:
    • Short-term, high-grade bonds are not significantly affected by market price changes.
    • Long-term bonds and common stocks are subject to wide price fluctuations over time.
  • Anticipating Market Fluctuations:
    • Attempting to anticipate market movements by timing (forecasting future course) is generally futile for investors.
    • Pricing (buying at fair values and selling above) is a more promising approach than timing.
    • Buy-low-sell-high approach worked well historically but became difficult after the 1949-50 market rise.
    • Formula investment plans that automatically sell on market rises can cause investors to miss out.
  • Fluctuations and the Intelligent Investor:
    • True investors should not be overly concerned with short-term market fluctuations.
    • Market quotes provide convenience for increasing/decreasing holdings, but are not binding.
    • Investors should view themselves as part-owners of businesses, not just stock traders.
    • The more successful a company, the greater the potential for large stock price fluctuations.
  • The A&P Company Example:
    • A&P's stock price fell extremely low in 1938 despite its strong business, demonstrating market irrationality.
    • The market can be wrong, allowing investors to profit from its mistakes.
    • Businesses change over time, so investors should re-evaluate companies periodically.
  • Mr. Market Analogy:
    • Investors should form their own independent opinion and not blindly follow "Mr. Market's" daily pricing.
    • Buy when prices are irrationally low and sell when irrationally high, but be patient otherwise.

Investing in Investment Funds

  • Mutual Fund Performance vs the Market:
    • Mutual funds as a group have performed roughly in line with broad market indexes over long periods.
    • It is difficult for any mutual fund to consistently outperform the market averages by a wide margin.
    • Top performing funds one year often fail to maintain that performance in subsequent years.
  • "Performance" Funds:
    • The cult of "performance" fueled unrealistic expectations and excessive risk-taking by some funds.
    • Many "performance" funds achieved spectacular short-term gains through speculation but later suffered major losses.
    • The intelligent investor should avoid funds promising miracles and chasing past performance.
  • Closed-End vs Open-End Funds:
    • Closed-end funds often sell at a discount to their net asset value, while open-end funds sell at a premium.
    • The intelligent investor should buy closed-end funds at a discount of 10-15% rather than pay a premium for open-end funds.
    • There is little evidence that premium-priced closed-end funds offer superior long-term performance.
  • Balanced Funds:
    • Balanced funds hold a mix of bonds and stocks, but often have low income yields.
    • Investors may be better off holding high-quality bonds directly rather than through a balanced fund.

The Investor and His Advisers

  • Reliance on Investment Advice:
    • Investing in securities often involves seeking advice from others.
    • Investors are amateurs, so they feel professional guidance can help them profit.
    • But seeking advice on how to make money has an element of naivety.
  • Sources of Investment Advice:
    • Relatives or friends presumed knowledgeable about securities.
    • Local (commercial) bankers.
    • Brokerage firms or investment banking houses.
    • Financial services or periodicals.
    • Investment counselors.
  • Investment Counsel and Trust Services:
    • Established investment counsel firms charge substantial fees.
    • They typically invest in standard interest/dividend-paying securities.
    • Aim to conserve principal and produce acceptable income.
    • Trust services of large banks operate similarly.
  • Financial Services:
    • Provide bulletins, market forecasts, and individual security advice.
    • Can be useful for information, but forecasts have little significance.
    • Security recommendations often lack thorough analysis.
  • Advice from Brokerage Houses:
    • Customers' brokers earn commissions, so can be speculation-minded.
    • Financial analysts study securities but may focus too much on near-term prospects.
    • Analysts can be valuable if the investor is value-minded, not quotation-minded.
  • Investment Bankers:
    • Underwrite and sell new securities issues.
    • Dealings with public investors involve an adviser-salesman conflict.
    • Investor should apply independent judgment, not blindly trust recommendations.
  • Other Advisers:
    • Local bankers can provide conservative, steadying influence.
    • Advice from relatives or friends is often unreliable.

Security Analysis for the Lay Investor: General Approach

  • Bond Analysis:
    • Primary tests for safety of bonds and preferred stocks are coverage of interest charges by earnings over past years.
    • Graham recommends specific "coverage" standards based on type of company for bonds to be considered investment grade.
    • Other tests include size of enterprise, equity cushion, property values (for utilities and real estate companies).
    • Past earnings coverage has proven a reliable guide for future bond safety and avoiding defaults.
  • Common Stock Analysis:
    • Ideal approach is to estimate average future earnings and apply an appropriate capitalization multiplier.
    • Factors affecting the multiplier include prospects, management, financial strength, dividend record/rate.
    • Formulas have been developed for valuing growth stocks based on expected growth rates, but have limitations.
    • Industry analysis can provide insights but Wall Street forecasts are often inaccurate.
  • Recommended Two-Part Appraisal:
    • First determine "past performance value" based solely on historical factors like profitability, stability, growth.
    • Then modify this value based on analysis of future factors expected to differ from the past.
    • Provides a systematic approach and useful experience, though valuations may still have limitations.

Things to Consider About Per-Share Earnings

  • Multiple Earnings Figures:
    • Companies often report multiple earnings per share figures (primary, diluted, before/after special charges, etc.)
    • Investors need to understand what each figure represents and use the appropriate one for analysis.
  • Special Charges:
    • Companies sometimes exclude costs as "special charges" to boost reported earnings.
    • Special charges may relate to legitimate future losses, but are often used opportunistically.
    • Investors should scrutinize special charges and consider them part of normal earnings.
  • Tax Benefits from Past Losses:
    • Past losses can reduce tax liabilities and inflate future earnings through tax credits.
    • Investors should consider reported earnings with and without tax benefits from losses.
  • Dilution from Convertibles:
    • Convertible securities like bonds or warrants can dilute earnings per share if converted.
    • Fully diluted earnings per share figures account for potential dilution.
  • Accounting Methods:
    • Depreciation methods, inventory valuation, R&D treatment also impact reported earnings.
    • Changes in accounting methods can cause earnings distortions year-to-year.
  • Use of Average Earnings:
    • Using multi-year average earnings can smooth out cyclical fluctuations.
    • Growth rate should be calculated to evaluate average earnings relevance.

Stock Selection for the Defensive Investor

  • Two Approaches:
    • DJIA-type portfolio: Buy a cross-section of leading blue-chip stocks, e.g. the 30 DJIA issues.
    • Quantitatively-tested portfolio: Apply a set of criteria to each stock purchase.
  • Seven Criteria for Quantitative Selection:
    • 1. Adequate Size: At least $100 million in sales for industrials; $50 million in assets for utilities.
    • 2. Strong Financial Condition: Current ratio at least 2:1; long-term debt less than working capital for industrials. Debt less than 2x equity for utilities.
    • 3. Earnings Stability: Some earnings for common stock in each of the past 10 years.
    • 4. Dividend Record: Uninterrupted dividends for at least 20 years.
    • 5. Earnings Growth: At least 33% increase in EPS over the past 10 years using 3-year averages.
    • 6. Moderate P/E Ratio: Current price no more than 15 times 3-year average earnings.
    • 7. Moderate Price/Book Ratio: Current price no more than 1.5 times book value. (Price/Book) x (P/E) should not exceed 22.5.
  • Commentary:
    • The criteria eliminate most companies as too small, weak finances, poor dividend record, or overpriced.
    • The modest growth requirement avoids including stagnant companies.
    • The P/E limit aims for an earnings yield as good as high-grade bond yields.
    • The defensive investor must judge if the criteria are too strict versus analysts' more generous recommendations.
In essence, Graham advocates a quantitative approach to build a portfolio of large, conservatively financed companies with continuous earnings, dividends, and moderate growth - purchased at reasonable prices based on earnings and assets. This allows the defensive investor to safely participate in stock market returns while minimizing the risk of permanent loss.

Stock Selection for the Defensive Investor

  • Seven Criteria for Selecting Defensive Stocks:
    • 1. Adequate size of enterprise (e.g. $100 million revenue for industrials)
    • 2. Sufficiently strong financial condition (e.g. current ratio over 2, manageable debt)
    • 3. Earnings stability (some earnings for common stock over last 10 years)
    • 4. Dividend record (uninterrupted dividends for at least 20 years)
    • 5. Earnings growth (minimum 33% increase in per-share earnings over last 10 years)
    • 6. Moderate price/earnings ratio (P/E under 15 for industrial stocks)
    • 7. Moderate ratio of price to assets (P/B ratio under 1.5, with restrictions)
  • Applying Criteria to Dow Jones Industrial Average in 1970:
    • Only 5 of 30 DJIA stocks met all 7 criteria (American Can, AT&T, Anaconda, Swift, Woolworth)
    • Around 100 listed industrial stocks may have qualified based on the criteria
  • The Public-Utility "Solution":
    • Vast majority of utility stocks met the criteria for performance and price in 1972
    • Offered good growth, earnings stability, higher dividend yields at lower P/E and P/B ratios
    • Advantages of regulated monopolies in raising capital and offsetting inflated costs
  • Investing in Financial Companies:
    • Financial condition more important due to high leverage and regulations
    • Broad performance similar to industrials from 1948-1970
    • Cautions about assessing soundness of financial companies

Stock Selection for the Enterprising Investor

  • Performance of Mutual Funds:
    • Mutual funds have generally failed to outperform broad market averages over long periods.
    • Possible reasons: market efficiency, flaws in analytical approach focused on growth prospects rather than value.
  • Graham-Newman Methods:
    • Arbitrages - Buying securities being exchanged in corporate reorganizations.
    • Liquidations - Buying shares of companies being liquidated.
    • Related hedges - Buying convertible issues and shorting the underlying common stock.
    • Net-current-asset bargains - Buying stocks at less than net current asset value.
  • Secondary Companies:
    • Use Standard & Poor's Stock Guide to screen for stocks meeting criteria like:
      • P/E ratio under 9
      • Current assets > 1.5x current liabilities
      • Debt < 110% of net current assets
      • No deficits in last 5 years
      • Dividend payments
      • Recent earnings growth
      • Price < 120% of net tangible assets
    • Applying these criteria can yield a list of 100+ potential bargain stocks.
  • Single Criteria Tests:
    • Tested performance of portfolios based on single criteria like low P/E, high dividend, etc.
    • Random stock portfolios underperformed market indexes.
    • Higher quality rankings and very large companies performed relatively well.
    • "Goodwill giants" with high intangible asset values also did well.
  • Net-Current-Asset Bargains:
    • Historically a successful approach when such bargains were available.
    • But opportunities have been limited in recent decades.

Convertible Issues and Warrants

  • Convertible Securities Overview:
    • Convertibles allow the issuer to raise capital at lower cost by offering a conversion privilege.
    • But investors often give up quality or yield when buying convertibles.
    • Convertibles issued late in bull markets tend to perform poorly when the market declines.
  • Issues With Owning Convertibles:
    • Deciding whether to sell after a small gain or hold for a bigger advance is difficult.
    • Holdings tend to have fully as many substantial losses as gains.
    • Convertibles perform poorly in declining markets compared to straight bonds.
  • Effect on Common Stock:
    • Convertible issues dilute current and future earnings per share of the common stock.
    • Fast-growing "conglomerates" have been major practitioners of this dilution.
    • In some cases, the convertible preferred is more attractive than the common stock.
  • Stock Warrants:
    • Warrants create huge market values "out of thin air" and serve no legitimate purpose.
    • They dilute and reduce the true value of the common stock.
    • Warrants fabricate imaginary market values and should be prohibited or limited.

A Comparison of Eight Pairs of Companies

  • Pair 1: Real Estate Investment Trust vs. Realty Equities Corp.
    • Real Estate Investment Trust was a conservatively managed enterprise, keeping debt low and growth moderate.
    • Realty Equities rapidly expanded assets and debt through diversification into various ventures, using complex financial instruments.
    • Realty Equities' overexpansion led to large losses, auditor issues, and a plummeting stock price.
  • Pair 2: Air Products and Chemicals vs. Air Reduction Co.
    • Air Products had higher profitability and growth but traded at a premium valuation.
    • Air Reduction had lower profitability but was undervalued compared to assets and earnings.
    • Choosing between the "quality" premium stock or the "bargain" value stock was difficult to judge definitively.
  • Pair 3: American Home Products Co. vs. American Hospital Supply Co.
    • Both were large, successful "health industry" companies with stable earnings growth.
    • Home Products had higher profitability and lower valuation multiples.
    • Hospital Supply had lower profitability but higher growth, leading to a much higher valuation multiple.
    • Neither met Graham's criteria for a sound, reasonably priced investment at the time.
  • Pair 4: H&R Block vs. Blue Bell
    • H&R Block had a "meteoric" rise but extremely high valuation over 100x earnings.
    • Blue Bell was a steadily growing company in a competitive industry but valued modestly.
    • The market's optimism about Block appeared excessive compared to the undervalued Blue Bell.
  • Pair 5: International Flavors & Fragrances vs. International Harvester
    • Flavors & Fragrances was a smaller but highly profitable "one-product" business.
    • Harvester was a conglomerate with low profitability despite massive sales volume.
    • Flavors & Fragrances' high valuation was justified by excellent profits, unlike Harvester's poor showing.
  • Pair 6: McGraw Edison vs. McGraw-Hill
    • McGraw-Hill had lower sales/earnings but higher valuation multiples due to investor enthusiasm.
    • The excessive valuation of McGraw-Hill seemed unjustified given declining profitability.
    • McGraw Edison appeared reasonably valued relative to its operating performance.
  • Pair 7: National General Corp. vs. National Presto Industries
    • National General was a large conglomerate with complex capital structure and high valuation.
    • National Presto was a simple operating company with modest valuation despite strong profits.
    • National General's high valuation seemed grossly inflated compared to its actual economics.
  • Pair 8: Whiting Corp. vs. Willcox & Gibbs
    • Whiting was a conservatively-run industrial company with steady profits and high dividend.
    • Willcox & Gibbs was a struggling conglomerate with low profits and no dividend.
    • Whiting seemed enormously undervalued relative to the overvalued Willcox & Gibbs.

Shareholders and Managements: Dividend Policy

  • Historical Dividend Debate:
    • Traditionally, shareholders wanted more liberal cash dividends while managements preferred retaining earnings for growth.
    • Managements argued shareholders should sacrifice present income for the company's long-term benefit.
  • Changing Attitudes Toward Dividends:
    • Today, growth companies often pay low dividends and retain earnings for profitable reinvestment.
    • Investors approve of low payout ratios for rapidly growing companies that can deploy capital effectively.
    • For slower-growth companies, the case for paying out a normal portion of earnings (e.g. two-thirds) is stronger.
  • Guidelines on Dividend Policy:
    • Shareholders should demand a normal payout or clear evidence that retained profits increase per-share earnings.
    • Low payouts due to weak financial condition are understandable, but expansion by struggling companies seldom benefits shareholders.
    • Detailed explanations and defenses are needed if an underperforming company retains most earnings for growth.
  • Stock Dividends vs Stock Splits:
    • A stock dividend represents capitalization of recent reinvested earnings into new shares.
    • A stock split is just a change in share structure to reset prices, with no surplus transfer.
    • Graham advocated periodic, clearly defined policies for stock dividends to avoid confusion.
The key points emphasize evaluating whether a company's dividend policy serves shareholder interests, with a balanced view allowing reinvestment for growth when productive while advocating shareholder rights to reasonable payouts from underperforming companies.

"Margin of Safety" as the Central Concept of Investment

  • Definition of Margin of Safety:
    • For bonds, it is the amount by which business assets or earnings exceed debt obligations, providing a cushion against potential losses.
    • For stocks, it is the difference between the stock's price and the estimated intrinsic value, with the price being lower than the value.
  • Importance of Margin of Safety:
    • It allows room for imprecision, inevitable mistakes, and unfavorable circumstances in calculating value.
    • It renders unnecessary an accurate estimate of the future for an investment to succeed.
    • It is the central concept of investment, distinguishing it from speculation.
  • Margin of Safety for Stocks:
    • Historically, the earnings yield on stocks was much higher than bond yields, providing a margin of safety.
    • Currently, with high stock prices, the margin of safety has been eroded or eliminated for many stocks.
    • Growth stocks tend to have inadequate margins of safety at market prices.
  • Undervalued Securities and Margin of Safety:
    • Bargain securities trading well below their value have a large margin of safety.
    • This makes them sound investments, despite being regarded as speculative by conventional standards.
  • Diversification and Margin of Safety:
    • Diversification increases the overall margin of safety by reducing the impact of mistakes.
    • It is based on the principle that the aggregate gains will exceed the aggregate losses.
  • Defining Investment vs. Speculation:
    • A true investment requires a provable margin of safety, demonstrated by figures and sound reasoning.
    • Speculation lacks a true margin of safety and rests largely on subjective judgments.
