Chapter One: The Top Secret Plan for General Nuclear War

  • Overview of the Meeting:
    • A secret plan discussed by American military officials in December 1960 at the Strategic Air Command Headquarters.
    • The plan involved a full-scale nuclear attack against the Soviet Union resulting in the death of 600 million people, one-fifth of the world's population at that time.
    • Key officials present included high-ranking military personnel and Deputy Director of Defense Research and Engineering John H. Rubel.
  • Revelation by John H. Rubel:
    • John H. Rubel, a firsthand witness of the meeting, revealed details of the plan in a memoir before his death in 2008.
    • Rubel expressed remorse for his involvement in the plan, referring to it as "mass extermination."
    • The plan involved multiple waves of nuclear attacks, with Moscow being a primary target for massive destruction.
  • The Shocking Details:
    • The plan called for a total of forty megatons on Moscow, significantly more destructive than the atomic bombs dropped during WWII.
    • Rubel's revelation marked the first known instance of a meeting attendee sharing personal accounts of the plan, highlighting the genocidal nature of the proposed nuclear war strategy.

The Buildup, Nuclear War

  • Los Alamos, Lawrence Livermore, and Sandia National Laboratories:
    • After World War II, Los Alamos lab faced uncertain future until the U.S. Navy sponsored atomic bomb tests, injecting new life into the atomic bomb program.
    • A report warned of the destructive power of atomic bombs but recommended stockpiling more weapons due to the threat of a surprise attack from Russia.
    • The race for nuclear supremacy intensified with the development of the hydrogen bomb, a thermonuclear weapon capable of immense destruction.
  • The Super:
    • In 1952, the thermonuclear bomb, or hydrogen bomb, was invented with unprecedented destructive power.
    • The first thermonuclear bomb test, Ivy Mike, obliterated an island and marked the beginning of a rapid increase in nuclear stockpiling by the U.S. and the Soviet Union.
    • The development of thermonuclear weapons led to industrialized production, with thousands of weapons being produced yearly.
  • Deterrence:
    • Nuclear war policies were shaped by the concept of deterrence, maintaining massive nuclear arsenals to discourage nuclear attacks.
    • War planners focused on building and defending against nuclear attacks under the guise of deterrence, leading to exorbitant spending on nuclear weapons.
    • The effectiveness of deterrence in preventing nuclear war was questioned, as the risk of failure raised concerns about the consequences of mass nuclear stockpiling.

The SIOP

  • Establishment of the SIOP:
    • Less than two weeks after WWII, the U.S. military requested 466 nuclear bombs for the stockpile.
    • Before 1960, each U.S. military force had its own nuclear war plans, leading to chaos.
    • The secretary of defense ordered the integration of all plans into the Single Integrated Operational Plan (SIOP).
  • Details of the SIOP:
    • Strategic Air Command had 280,000 employees working on the new plan.
    • The SIOP detailed a preemptive nuclear strike on Moscow, with estimated casualties of 600 million people, including millions in neighboring countries.
    • General David M. Shoup was the only dissenter against the plan, criticizing the indiscriminate killing of millions.
  • Comparison to Historical Events:
    • John Rubel likened the SIOP meeting to the Nazi's Wannsee Conference planning the genocide of millions.
    • Both events involved rational individuals deciding on mass extermination strategies.
