Book Summary: The Little Book of Common Sense Investing

A Parable

  • The Gotrocks Family:
    • A wealthy family owning 100% of every stock in the U.S.
    • Each year, they reaped all the earnings growth and dividends from the corporations.
    • All family members grew wealthier at the same pace.
  • The Arrival of Helpers:
    • Helpers (brokers) convinced some "smart" cousins to buy and sell stocks among themselves.
    • The Helpers received commissions, slowing the family's wealth growth.
    • Stock trading also generated capital gains taxes, further diminishing wealth.
  • Hiring Money Managers:
    • The cousins hired stock-picking experts (money managers) to gain an advantage.
    • The managers charged fees, further reducing the family's share of the pie.
    • Excessive trading by managers increased brokerage commissions and taxes.
  • Hiring Consultants:
    • Failing to pick good managers, the cousins hired consultants to advise on manager selection.
    • The consultants charged fees, further diminishing the family's returns.
  • The Wise Uncle's Advice:
    • A wise uncle advised getting rid of all the Helpers to reclaim 100% of the pie.
    • This is what index funds do - hold all stocks passively with minimal costs.
    • Successful investing is about owning businesses and reaping rewards through dividends and earnings growth.

Rational Exuberance

  • Shareholders Gains Must Match Business Gains
    • Over the long run, returns earned by shareholders must match returns earned by the underlying businesses they own.
    • Investment returns come from dividends and earnings growth, while speculative returns come from changes in price/earnings (P/E) ratios driven by investor emotions.
    • Since 1900, investment returns (earnings growth + dividend yield) averaged 9% annually, while speculative returns added only 0.5% to total market returns of 9.5%.
  • The Dual Nature of Stock Returns
    • Stock returns have two components: investment returns (enterprise) and speculative returns (market sentiment).
    • Investment returns from dividends and earnings growth are relatively steady, while speculative returns from changing P/E ratios are volatile.
    • Periods of high/low speculative returns tend to revert to the mean over time.
  • Reversion to the Mean
    • Stock prices often deviate from fundamental values, but eventually revert back to being priced based on business realities.
    • Decades of high speculative returns are followed by decades of low speculative returns, and vice versa.
  • The Real Market vs Expectations Market
    • The "real market" is the business world of products, services, profits and dividends.
    • The "expectations market" is where stock prices are set by speculation on future expectations, often disconnected from business realities.
    • Investors should focus on the "real market" of businesses, not the distracting speculation of the stock market.

Cast Your Lot with Business

  • Occam's Razor:
    • When there are multiple solutions to a problem, choose the simplest one.
    • The simplest way to own all U.S. businesses is to hold the total stock market portfolio.
  • Standard & Poor's 500 Index (S&P 500):
    • Represents 500 largest U.S. corporations, weighted by market capitalization.
    • Has been the accepted stock market portfolio for the past 90 years.
    • Serves as a benchmark for evaluating performance of professional fund managers.
  • Dow Jones Wilshire Total Stock Market Index:
    • Includes around 3,599 stocks, including the S&P 500 stocks.
    • Best measure of the aggregate value and returns of U.S. stocks.
    • Returns of S&P 500 and Total Stock Market Index are nearly identical over long periods.
  • Index Funds Outperform Active Funds:
    • SPIVA report shows 90% of actively managed funds underperformed relevant indexes over 15 years.
    • Vanguard 500 Index Fund (started in 1976) had an outstanding long-term performance.
    • Owning a diversified, low-cost index fund is the simplest and most productive solution.

How Most Investors Turn a Winner's Game into a Loser's Game

  • The Arithmetic of Investing
    • Investors as a group must necessarily earn the market return before costs are deducted.
    • After accounting for the costs of investing (management fees, trading costs, etc.), the returns of investors fall short of the market return by the aggregate amount of those costs.
    • Before costs, beating the market is a zero-sum game. After costs, it becomes a loser's game.
  • The Impact of Costs
    • The costs of investing for individual investors (trading costs) can average 1.5% or more per year.
    • For actively managed mutual funds, expense ratios average around 1.3%, plus additional costs like sales charges and portfolio turnover costs.
    • These costs compound over time, leading to a significant gap between investor returns and market returns over long periods.
  • The Relentless Rules of Arithmetic
    • The chapter references Louis D. Brandeis, who warned about the "relentless rules of humble arithmetic" in investing.
    • Most investors struggle to recognize the obvious reality of how costs erode returns over time.
    • Financial intermediaries have little incentive to highlight this reality, as it goes against their self-interest.
  • The Tyranny of Compounding Costs
    • An example shows how a 2% annual cost gap leads to a dramatic difference in returns over 50 years ($294,600 vs. $114,700 on a $10,000 investment).
    • Over long periods, the miracle of compounding returns is overwhelmed by the tyranny of compounding costs.
    • Investors who put up all the capital and take all the risk end up with a small fraction of the potential market return.

Focus on the Lowest-Cost Funds

  • Past Performance is Not a Reliable Indicator:
    • Most investors rely heavily on a fund's past performance when selecting funds.
    • However, past performance is not predictive of future performance.
  • Costs Are the Key Factor Shaping Returns:
    • A fund's costs are a powerful and often ignored factor that shapes its returns.
    • The main costs are the expense ratio, sales loads, and portfolio turnover costs.
    • These costs tend to persist over time for each fund.
  • Lower Costs Lead to Higher Returns:
    • Studies consistently show that lower-cost funds outperform higher-cost funds.
    • The difference in costs largely explains the performance gap between low and high-cost funds.
    • Over 25 years, the lowest-cost quartile funds averaged 9.4% annual returns, while the highest-cost quartile averaged just 8.3%.
  • Focus on Minimizing Total Costs:
    • Instead of chasing past performance, investors should focus on selecting the lowest-cost funds.
    • Minimizing the total costs (expense ratios, loads, and turnover) is key to achieving higher net returns.

Dividends Are the Investor's (Best?) Friend

  • Dividends' Significant Contribution:
    • Since 1926, dividends have contributed an average annual return of 4.2%, accounting for 42% of the stock market's 10% annual return.
    • Reinvesting dividends leads to significantly higher returns - a $10,000 investment in 1926 would have grown to $59.1 million with dividends reinvested, compared to $1.7 million from price appreciation alone.
  • Stability of Dividends:
    • S&P 500 dividends per share have remained remarkably stable over 90 years, with only a few significant drops during crises like the Great Depression and 2008 financial crisis.
    • Dividends per share reached a new high of $45.70 in 2016, 60% above the previous peak in 2008.
  • Mutual Funds Confiscate Dividends:
    • Actively managed mutual funds, whose fees are based on net assets rather than dividend income, consume a large portion of the dividends earned.
    • In 2016, actively managed growth funds consumed 100% of dividend income through expenses, while value funds consumed 58%.
    • In contrast, low-cost index funds consumed only 2-4% of dividend income through expenses.
  • The Case for Index Funds:
    • Index funds have low expense ratios (as low as 0.04%) and deliver a fair share of dividend income to investors.
    • They avoid the confiscation of dividends by active managers and the excessive trading costs associated with active management.

The Grand Illusion

  • Mutual Fund Returns vs. Investor Returns:
    • Over the past 25 years, the average mutual fund earned a return of 7.8% per year, lower than the 9.1% return of the S&P 500.
    • However, the average fund investor earned only 6.3% per year, 1.5 percentage points lower than the fund return itself.
  • The Dual Penalties:
    • Investors incur a cost penalty from mutual fund expenses.
    • They also incur a behavioral penalty due to counterproductive market timing and adverse fund selection.
  • Counterproductive Investor Behavior:
    • Investors invested too little in equity funds when stocks were undervalued in the 1980s and early 1990s.
    • They poured excessive amounts into equity funds, especially riskier ones, near the market peak in the late 1990s.
    • Many investors sold near the market bottom during the 2008-2009 financial crisis, missing out on the subsequent recovery.
  • Fund Industry Promotions:
    • The fund industry compounds the problem by heavily marketing new, often riskier funds and advertising past returns of hot funds.
    • This plays on investors' emotions and leads to counterproductive behavior.
  • The Case for Index Funds:
    • Index funds eliminate the temptation of chasing hot funds and remove emotions from the equation.
    • The winning formula is owning the entire stock market through an index fund and staying the course for the long term.

Taxes Are Costs, Too

  • Tax Inefficiency of Active Funds:
    • Active funds have high portfolio turnover (avg. 78% per year), leading to frequent realization of capital gains.
    • This results in higher tax burdens for investors in active funds compared to index funds.
    • High turnover is driven by active funds' short-term speculation rather than long-term investing.
  • Impact of Taxes on Returns:
    • After accounting for taxes, the average actively managed equity fund had an annual after-tax return of 6.6% over the past 25 years.
    • The index fund had an annual after-tax return of 8.6% over the same period.
    • A $10,000 investment in active funds grew to $39,700 after taxes, compared to $68,300 for index funds - a $28,600 difference.
  • Index Fund Tax Efficiency:
    • Index funds have very low portfolio turnover (around 3% per year), minimizing realized capital gains.
    • While index funds are tax-efficient on capital gains, they distribute more dividend income which is taxable.
    • However, the overall tax burden is still lower for index fund investors.
  • Expert Views:
    • The chapter cites experts like John Shoven, William Bernstein, and Burton Malkiel highlighting the tax advantages of index funds.

When the Good Times No Longer Roll

  • Lower Future Returns Expected for Stocks and Bonds
    • Historical stock returns were driven by earnings growth and speculative P/E expansion.
    • Future stock returns likely around 4% (6% investment return - 2% speculative return).
    • Bond returns closely tied to initial yield, currently around 3.1% for a diversified bond portfolio.
  • Balanced Portfolio Outlook
    • A 60/40 stock/bond portfolio expected to return 3.6% before costs and inflation.
    • After typical 1.5% costs for active management and 2% inflation, real return near 0.1%.
    • Low-cost index funds could provide a 1.5% real return in this environment.
  • Implications of Lower Returns
    • High costs severely erode already low expected returns for active funds.
    • Avoiding financial devastation requires low-cost index funds or rock-bottom-cost active funds.
    • Picking winning funds based on past performance is unlikely to succeed.
  • Expert Projections Align
    • Many economists and analysts also forecast subdued investment returns ahead.
    • Projected returns range from slightly positive to negative for stocks and bonds.
    • High fees are deemed illogical and unsustainable in this environment.

Selecting Long-Term Winners

  • Don't Look for the Needleâ€”Buy the Haystack:
    • It is extremely difficult to select winning mutual funds in advance based on past performance.
    • Out of 355 equity funds that existed in 1970, only 10 (2.8%) outperformed the S&P 500 by more than 1% annually over the next 46 years.
    • 281 funds (79.2%) went out of business, mostly due to poor performance.
  • Fund Survival and Manager Turnover:
    • Only 74 out of the original 355 funds survived the 46-year period.
    • Average tenure of active equity fund portfolio managers is only around 9 years.
    • Successful funds often attract large inflows, making it harder to outperform in the future.
  • The Magellan and Contrafund Stories:
    • Fidelity Magellan and Contrafund were two of the few long-term outperformers, but their advantages diminished as assets grew.
    • Magellan's outperformance declined as assets reached over $100 billion.
    • Contrafund's recent outperformance has waned as assets crossed $100 billion.
  • The Case for Indexing:
    • Trying to select outperforming funds is like "looking for a needle in a haystack."
    • A low-cost index fund would have matched or exceeded the returns of 97.2% of funds over the 46-year period.
    • Investing in one index fund for a lifetime is simpler, more cost-efficient, and avoids the challenges of manager turnover and fund closures.
  • Expert Advice on Indexing:
    • Warren Buffett recommends a low-cost S&P 500 index fund for his wife's trust.
    • Paul Samuelson advises against the futile search for superior fund managers.
    • Financial columnists echo the difficulty of picking winners and recommend index funds.

Reversion to the Mean

  • Short-Term Fund Performance is Poor Predictor of Future Success:
    • Over 150% of investor cash flows went into funds rated 4-5 stars by Morningstar in 2016, based heavily on short-term performance.
    • But only 14% of 5-star funds in 2004 retained that rating a decade later, with 50% dropping to 3 stars or fewer.
  • Lack of Persistence in Fund Returns:
    • Among top quintile funds from 2006-2011, only 13% remained top quintile in 2011-2016, while 27% fell to bottom quintile.
    • But 17% of bottom quintile funds from 2006-2011 became top quintile in 2011-2016.
    • Similar lack of persistence seen in 2001-2006 and 2006-2011 periods.
  • Reversion to the Mean is Powerful:
    • Fund returns tend to revert to the average or below after periods of exceptional performance.
    • Relative returns appear largely random, with steady reversion centering around 16% in each quintile.
    • Up to 18% of funds failed to survive the subsequent 5-year period, likely due to poor performance.
  • Investor Bias and Chasing Performance:
    • Investors tend to chase recent winners, biased towards causal explanations over statistics.
    • Past performance is not predictive of future results - "the math" shows this clearly.
    • Chasing past winners is called one of the "stupidest things an investor can do" by experts.

Seeking Advice to Select Funds?

  • The Evidence on Selecting Winning Funds:
    • Selecting winning equity funds for the long-term is extremely difficult, like finding a needle in a haystack.
    • Selecting funds based on short-term past performance often leads to disappointment.
  • Relying on Professional Advice:
    • Many investors seek advice from financial consultants (stockbrokers), registered investment advisers (RIAs), or insurance agents.
    • RIAs can provide valuable services like asset allocation guidance, tax considerations, and general financial advice.
    • However, there is no evidence that advisers as a group can consistently select funds that outperform the market.
  • Evidence on Advisers' Performance:
    • A study found that funds recommended by advisers underperformed those purchased directly, costing investors $9 billion per year.
    • Merrill Lynch's "Focus Twenty" and "Internet Strategies" funds were marketing successes but investment disasters, with clients losing up to 86% of assets.
  • The Value of Advisers:
    • Advisers can provide peace of mind, help match risk tolerance, navigate complexities, and keep clients disciplined.
    • However, advisers' fees can significantly reduce returns over time, so their value must be weighed against the cost.
    • Advisers who recommend low-cost index funds in model portfolios are favored.
  • The Fiduciary Standard:
    • There is a trend towards establishing a federal fiduciary duty for advisers, requiring them to put clients' interests first.
    • RIAs already have a fiduciary duty, but the standard would be extended to stockbrokers and insurance agents.
    • The principle of fiduciary duty is expected to prevail, even if current proposals face challenges.

Profit from the Majesty of Simplicity and Parsimony

  • Hold Traditional Low-Cost Index Funds That Track the Stock Market:
    • Lessons from previous chapters:
      • Costs matter
      • Selecting funds based on past performance doesn't work
      • Fund returns revert to the mean
      • Relying on advice works only sporadically
    • Traditional index funds (TIFs) that own the entire stock market have the lowest costs (expense ratios as low as 0.04%)
    • Monte Carlo simulation shows that over longer periods (50 years), only 2% of active managers would be expected to outperform passive index funds
    • Index funds deserve an important place in your portfolio due to their simplicity and low costs
  • All Index Funds Are Not Created Equal:
    • Costs vary widely among index funds tracking the same index
    • Gap of 1.3% in expense ratios between low-cost and high-cost S&P 500 index funds
    • High-cost funds also charge front-end sales loads
    • Example: Vanguard 500 Index (0.04% expense ratio) grew $10,000 to $294,900 over 33 years, while higher-cost Wells Fargo fund grew to only $232,100
    • Select the lowest-cost index funds from reputable fund organizations
  • Indexing Works in All Market Segments:
    • Conventional wisdom says indexing works for efficient markets like S&P 500, but active management may have an edge in less efficient markets
    • Data shows indexing works well in all market segments, whether efficient or inefficient
    • International and emerging market indexes outperformed majority of active funds
  • Caution About Gambling on Sector Funds:
    • Betting on winning market sectors is gambling, not investing
    • Emotions negatively impact returns investors achieve in sector funds
    • Most popular sector funds are those with recent spectacular performance, leading to poor timing
    • Owning a traditional low-cost stock index fund is the ultimate strategy with mathematical certainty

Bond Funds

  • The Relentless Rules of Arithmetic Apply:
    • The rules of humble arithmetic apply even more forcefully to bond funds than stock funds.
    • A single factor - prevailing interest rates - dominates bond returns.
    • Bond fund managers cannot influence interest rates set by the market.
  • Why Hold Bonds?
    • Stocks have historically provided higher long-term returns than bonds.
    • But bonds can outperform stocks in shorter periods and reduce portfolio volatility.
    • Current bond yields (3.1%) exceed stock dividend yields (2%), providing income advantage.
  • Bond Fund Manager Performance:
    • As a group, bond fund managers track the bond market, delivering gross returns in line with interest rates.
    • After expenses and fees, bond fund returns lag appropriate bond indexes.
    • 85% of actively managed bond funds underperformed indexes over 15 years (2001-2016).
  • Types of Bond Funds:
    • Short-term: Lower yield, reduced volatility risk.
    • Long-term: Higher yield, increased volatility risk.
    • Intermediate-term: Balance between yield and volatility.
  • Index Bond Funds:
    • Total bond market index funds track high-quality bond indexes (e.g. Barclays Aggregate).
    • Provide broad diversification, low costs, tax efficiency similar to stock index funds.
    • Outperform majority of active bond funds due to lower costs.

The Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)

  • ETF as a "Wolf in Sheep's Clothing":
    • Designed to facilitate trading, contrary to the buy-and-hold principles of traditional index funds (TIFs).
    • Offers less diversification by focusing on sectors or factors, increasing risk compared to broad market exposure.
    • Higher costs from trading commissions and taxes erode returns, contradicting the low-cost paradigm of TIFs.
  • The Creation of the "Spider":
    • First U.S. ETF, tracking the S&P 500 Index, launched in 1993.
    • Offered potential competition to traditional S&P 500 Index funds, but with trading flexibility.
    • Remains the largest ETF with over $240 billion in assets, but high trading volume and turnover.
  • ETF Growth and Diversity:
    • ETFs now account for 50% of all index fund assets, with over 2,000 ETFs available.
    • Offerings span broad market, sector, smart beta, leveraged, and other specialized strategies.
    • Investor cash flows into ETFs are highly volatile compared to TIFs.
  • Challenges with ETFs:
    • Investors in concentrated ETFs often chase past returns, leading to underperformance.
    • Costs from trading commissions and taxes compound over time, eroding returns.
    • Encourages short-term speculation and market timing rather than long-term investing.
  • Warnings and Concerns:
    • ETFs benefit entrepreneurs, brokers, and fund managers more than investors.
    • Potential for harm increases as offerings become more specialized and complex.
    • Straying from broad diversification and low costs contradicts the principles of indexing.

Index Funds That Promise to Beat the Market

  • Traditional Index Funds
    • Traditional index funds tracking broad market indexes like the S&P 500 have been highly successful, providing better returns than actively managed funds.
    • Assets in traditional index funds have grown massively as investors recognize their benefits.
  • New "Smart Beta" Index Funds
    • New index funds claim to beat the market by tracking indexes weighted by factors like value, momentum, dividends, etc. instead of market cap.
    • These "smart beta" funds are marketed as a new paradigm and revolution in indexing that can provide higher returns.
    • However, their long-term ability to outperform is unproven and defies market efficiency principles.
  • Skepticism Towards Smart Beta
    • The author expresses strong skepticism towards claims that smart beta strategies can consistently beat the market.
    • Data shows smart beta funds have performed similarly to cap-weighted indexes over 10 years.
    • Quotes from finance experts like Mankiw, Sharpe, and Siegel warn against buying into new indexing paradigms.
  • Sticking to Traditional Indexing
    • The author advocates sticking to traditional cap-weighted index funds that capture full market returns.
    • Chasing new strategies promising market-beating returns is unlikely to work out well in the long run.

What Would Benjamin Graham Have Thought about Indexing?

  • Graham's Advice for Defensive Investors:
    • Most investors should be "defensive" and settle for the market's return rather than trying to beat it.
    • Advocated owning a diversified portfolio and holding for the long-term to earn dividends and growth.
    • Cautioned against expecting too much from advisors and Wall Street.
  • Index Funds Align with Graham's Philosophy:
    • Graham described an investment approach very similar to index funds before they existed.
    • He acknowledged picking winning stocks was increasingly difficult over time.
    • In 1976, he stated the average manager cannot outperform the market indexes.
  • Graham Would Have Endorsed Index Funds:
    • Graham saw no reason why investors should accept inferior returns from active management.
    • He believed superior returns were harder than they looked, while capturing market returns was easier.
    • Warren Buffett stated Graham endorsed low-cost index funds for most investors.

Asset Allocation I: Stocks and Bonds

  • Significance of Asset Allocation:
    • An academic study found asset allocation accounts for 94% of differences in portfolio returns.
    • More important than fund selection, asset allocation between stocks and bonds is a crucial decision.
  • Benjamin Graham's 50/50 Stock/Bond Allocation:
    • Recommended 25-75% stocks, 75-25% bonds, with 50/50 as the standard division.
    • Allowed conservative investors to benefit from stock gains while limiting downside risk.
    • Based on stock yield premiums over bonds in his era (6.9% vs 1.9%).
  • Factors Influencing Asset Allocation:
    • Ability to take risk (financial position, future liabilities, time horizon).
    • Willingness to take risk (personal risk tolerance).
    • Younger investors can be more aggressive (e.g., 80% stocks), older investors more conservative (e.g., 25% stocks).
  • Key Asset Allocation Decisions:
    • Strategically allocating between stocks and bonds based on individual circumstances.
    • Maintaining a fixed ratio or allowing it to vary with market returns.
    • Whether to employ tactical changes in allocation based on market conditions.
    • Using actively managed funds or low-cost index funds.
  • Cost Impact on Asset Allocation:
    • Lower-cost portfolios may earn higher net returns than higher-risk, high-cost portfolios.
    • Cost is a critical factor to consider when deciding stock/bond allocations.

Asset Allocation II

  • Balanced Index Fund Success:
    • The 60/40 balanced index fund (60% stocks, 40% bonds) has been an extraordinary success.
    • Over 25 years, it earned 8.0% annually versus 6.3% for its peers, a 1.7% yearly advantage.
    • Its low 0.14% expense ratio and high correlation with peers suggest continued outperformance.
  • Retirement Income Strategy:
    • During retirement, focus on steady income rather than portfolio value fluctuations.
    • Combine Social Security payments with dividends from index funds for growing retirement income.
    • Consider using fund capital withdrawals in addition to dividends to meet income needs.
  • Non-U.S. Stocks in Portfolios:
    • The traditional two-fund model (U.S. stocks and bonds) has given way to a three-fund model with non-U.S. stocks.
    • Though U.S. stocks outperformed over the past 25 years, more global diversification may be warranted going forward.
  • Target-Date and LifeStrategy Funds:
    • These funds offer diversified portfolios with stocks/bonds adjusted for investor goals and timeframes.
    • Target-date funds automatically become more conservative as the target retirement date nears.
    • Consider low-cost index target-date funds over higher-cost actively managed versions.
  • Social Security in Asset Allocation:
    • Social Security acts as a bond-like asset and should be accounted for in asset allocation.
    • Deferring Social Security increases eventual monthly payments but means foregoing income for years.
    • Properly accounting for Social Security may call for a higher stock allocation than expected.
